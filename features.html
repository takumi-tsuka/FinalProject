origin of UDON

Udon is said to have originated in China. There is a theory that it was introduced via the Silk Road to the west as pasta and then to Japan as udon. It is believed to have changed depending on the local environment.

how did it come?

There is a theory that udon was introduced from China during the Nara period (710-794) and was called "udon" after a snack called "konton". Another popular theory is that a Japanese Buddhist monk named Kukai went to China to study and introduced udon to the Chinese people when he returned.

how did it change?

It is said that before the Edo period(1603-1867), udon was shaped like crushed dumplings, but it is believed that during the Edo period, udon took its present string-like form. A study of udon in various parts of Japan shows that many of them took on their current udon shape during the Edo period.

Modern Udon

Tempura with various ingredients, egg and egg topping, etc. were developed in the mid to late Edo period, and are still loved by the Japanese as a food for the common people, unchanged from the Edo period.

composition of UDON

Udon noodles, as mentioned above, are made from high-quality wheat, pure water, and mineral-rich salt. Udon noodles are eaten all over Japan, but most regions, especially famous ones, have these conditions in place. Udon noodles are very easy to digest, and it is said that eating udon noodles when you have a cold or are sick will make you feel better. The soup used for udon is made from something called ”dashi”. Dashi is made from seafood and other ingredients. Dashi soup stock contains a large amount of ”umami”, a nutrient that affects the sense of taste along with sweetness, saltiness, acidity, and bitterness. When soup stock made from various ingredients is combined, the umami taste is further increased. This is what enhances the taste of udon.

variety of UDON

Each region of Japan has its own udon, which is unique to that region. Here are some typical local udon. The ingredients are made from wheat, water, and salt, but they are completely different depending on how they are cut, how firm they are, and how they are kneaded. Udon is eaten in completely different ways in different parts of the country. Furthermore, the type of Dashi and the way it is made differs depending on the specialty, so we encourage you to compare different types of udon when you come to Japan.

UDON DAY

There is "UDON DAY" is Japan. In Japan. It is July 2, the 11th day after the summer solstice. In Japan, agriculture has been prosperous since ancient times, and there was a genetic belief that if farmers did not complete their work by about 10 days after the summer solstice, the harvest would be cut in half. The custom of rewarding the farmers for their hard work was born.
